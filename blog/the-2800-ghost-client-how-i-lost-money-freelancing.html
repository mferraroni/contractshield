The $2,800 Ghost Client: How a Handshake Deal Cost Me Three Weeks of Work

By ContractShield · 4 min read · May 27, 2026
← Back to Blog

The brief came in through LinkedIn. A product manager at a Series A startup. He wanted a full brand identity — logo system, color palette, typography, brand guidelines document. The timeline was tight: three weeks. He'd worked with designers before and seemed sharp. We got on a call, aligned on direction, and he said "let's go."

No contract. Just a Slack message that said "confirmed, let's get started."

I should have stopped there.

Three weeks later, I delivered everything: logo in 12 formats, a 40-page brand guide, motion guidelines, and a complete asset library. He went quiet. One day, two days. I followed up. No response. A week later I called the company — he'd left. The new contact said they hadn't commissioned any branding work and weren't paying for anything they didn't authorize.

$2,800. Gone.

The worst part wasn't the money. It was that I had no recourse. No contract meant no legal agreement. No deposit meant no skin in the game for the client. No IP clause meant I'd handed over work I technically still owned — but couldn't use, because it was custom to their brand.

I've talked to hundreds of freelancers since building ContractShield, and this story is shockingly common. The names change. The amounts change. The industry changes. But the pattern is identical: a confident client, a verbal agreement, no paperwork, and a bill that never gets paid.

What I'd Do Differently

First, I'd require a 50% deposit before starting anything. Not because I distrust every client — but because money creates commitment. A client who won't pay a deposit is telling you something important about how they treat vendors.

Second, I'd have a contract that explicitly states: "IP ownership transfers upon receipt of final payment in full." This means the client doesn't legally own the work until they've paid. That's leverage. That's recourse.

Third, I'd include a late payment clause — something like "invoices unpaid after 14 days accrue a 1.5% monthly late fee." Clients who know this pay faster.

None of this requires a lawyer. A well-structured freelance contract covers all three in two pages. It's the difference between a story like mine and one that ends differently.
